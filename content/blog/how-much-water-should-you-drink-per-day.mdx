"Drink eight glasses of water a day" is one of the most repeated pieces of health advice in the world. It is also a rule with surprisingly little science behind the specific number. The real answer to how much water you need depends on your body, your activity, and your environment — and it is worth knowing how to estimate it properly.

## Where the eight-glasses rule came from

The "8 by 8" rule — eight 8-ounce glasses, about 2 litres — is easy to remember and roughly the right order of magnitude. But it was never the conclusion of a careful study. It is closer to a slogan that stuck.

A frequently cited origin is a 1945 US recommendation that adults need about 2.5 litres of water a day. The part everyone forgot to quote was the next sentence: most of that water is already present in the food you eat. The "drink 2 litres on top of meals" version is a distortion of the original advice.

## What the evidence actually suggests

National health bodies give ranges, not a single magic number. A commonly cited reference from the US National Academies suggests total daily water intake of roughly:

- **About 3.7 litres for men**
- **About 2.7 litres for women**

The crucial detail: that is **total water from all sources** — drinks *and* food. Fruit, vegetables, soup, milk, coffee and tea all count. Food typically supplies around 20% of total intake. So the amount you need to actively *drink* is meaningfully less than the headline figure.

## What changes your number

Your personal target is not fixed. It moves with:

- **Body size.** A larger body holds and uses more water. This is why weight-based estimates exist — a common rough guide is 30–35 ml of water per kilogram of body weight.
- **Activity.** Exercise that makes you sweat can add 0.5–1 litre or more per hour of effort.
- **Climate.** Hot, dry or high-altitude environments increase water loss substantially.
- **Health status.** Fever, illness, pregnancy and breastfeeding all raise requirements.
- **Diet.** A diet high in water-rich produce supplies more water through food; a salty or high-protein diet increases need.

Because of all this, a single universal number is the wrong model. A range you adjust day to day is the right one. ## A practical way to estimate your target

A reasonable starting point for an average adult in a temperate climate:

1. Take your body weight in kilograms.
2. Multiply by 30–35 to get millilitres.
3. That is your rough *total* water target for a normal day.
4. Add roughly 500–700 ml for every hour of sweaty exercise.
5. Remember that food covers around a fifth of the total — so the amount to drink is the target minus what your meals provide.

A 70 kg adult lands around 2.1–2.5 litres total, of which perhaps 1.7–2 litres comes from drinks. That is close to the old rule — which is why the rule survived despite its shaky origins.

## The most reliable signal: your own body

You do not need to track millilitres. Two everyday signals are more useful than any formula:

- **Urine colour.** Pale straw yellow means well hydrated. Dark amber means drink more. Completely clear, all day, can mean you are overdoing it.
- **Thirst.** For healthy adults, thirst is a genuinely good guide. The idea that you are "already dehydrated by the time you feel thirsty" is overstated for normal daily life.

## Can you drink too much?

Yes, though it is rare. Drinking very large volumes in a short time can dilute blood sodium dangerously — a condition called hyponatraemia. It mostly affects endurance athletes who over-drink during long events. For ordinary daily life it is not a concern, but it is a reason to be sceptical of "force yourself to drink gallons" advice.

## The short version

There is no universal magic number. Total daily water need is roughly 2.7 litres for women and 3.7 for men — *including* the water in food — and it shifts with size, activity, climate and health. Estimate a personal range with a weight-based figure, then let urine colour and thirst fine-tune it day to day.
