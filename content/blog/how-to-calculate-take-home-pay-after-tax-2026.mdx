Knowing how to calculate your take-home pay after tax is essential whether you are negotiating a salary, comparing job offers, or budgeting for the year. Your gross salary — the headline number on the offer letter — is never what lands in your bank account. The gap is taxes and deductions, and it varies a lot by country.

## Gross pay vs net pay

**Gross pay** is your total salary before anything is removed. **Net pay** — your take-home pay — is what remains after taxes and deductions. In most countries the gap is 20–35% of gross. Always budget on net pay, never gross.

## United States: the deductions stack

In the US, several layers come out of each paycheck.

**Federal income tax** is progressive — different slices of income are taxed at rising rates. You do not pay your top rate on your whole salary, only on the portion inside that bracket.

**FICA** is a flat payroll tax: 6.2% for Social Security (up to an annual wage cap) and 1.45% for Medicare, totalling **7.65%** on most income.

**State income tax** varies enormously. Some states (Texas, Florida, Washington and others) have **no state income tax**; others charge up to ~13% at high incomes. This single factor can swing your take-home pay by thousands.

**Pre-tax deductions** — 401(k) contributions, health insurance premiums — reduce your taxable income, lowering the tax you pay while still leaving the workplace.

## United Kingdom: PAYE, NI and the personal allowance

The UK collects tax through **PAYE** (Pay As You Earn), straight from your payslip.

You get a **personal allowance** — a slice of income taxed at 0%. Above it, income tax runs through basic, higher and additional rate bands.

**National Insurance** is the UK's equivalent of payroll tax, charged above a threshold.

UK earners also typically see **pension auto-enrolment** contributions and, if applicable, student loan repayments deducted automatically.

## Canada: federal plus provincial

Canada stacks **federal** income tax and **provincial/territorial** income tax — and provincial rates differ widely, so where you live changes your net pay.

On top of income tax, employees pay **CPP** (Canada Pension Plan) and **EI** (Employment Insurance) contributions, both with annual maximums. A **basic personal amount** shields the first slice of income from federal tax.

## Why the same salary pays differently

A $100,000 salary does not produce the same take-home pay across these countries — or even within them. The drivers:

- **Where you live** — US state, Canadian province, or UK-wide rates.
- **Pre-tax contributions** — pension or 401(k) money lowers taxable income.
- **Filing status and credits** — marital status, dependants and tax credits all shift the result.
- **The wage caps** — Social Security, CPP and NI thresholds change the marginal deduction at higher incomes.

This is why "negotiate on net, not gross" matters when comparing offers in different locations.

## A simple way to estimate it

To rough out your take-home pay:

1. Start with gross annual salary.
2. Subtract pre-tax deductions (pension, health premiums) to get taxable income.
3. Apply income tax band by band — never your top rate on the whole amount.
4. Subtract payroll tax (FICA / NI / CPP + EI).
5. Subtract state or provincial tax where it applies.
6. What remains is your annual net pay; divide by 12 for monthly take-home.

## Frequently asked questions

**What is take-home pay?**
Take-home pay (net pay) is what you actually receive after income tax, payroll tax and deductions are removed from your gross salary.

**Why is my paycheck so much smaller than my salary?**
Income tax, payroll tax (FICA, NI or CPP/EI), and deductions like health insurance and retirement contributions all come out before you are paid.

**Do 401(k) or pension contributions reduce my tax?**
Yes — pre-tax retirement contributions lower your taxable income, so you pay less tax while still saving.

**Which US states have no income tax?**
Several, including Texas, Florida and Washington. Living in one meaningfully increases your take-home pay.

**How do I compare two job offers in different places?**
Convert each gross salary to net pay using the local tax rules, then compare the net figures — the gross numbers can be misleading.
