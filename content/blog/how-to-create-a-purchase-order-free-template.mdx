A purchase order (PO) is the document a buyer sends a supplier to formally authorize an order — quantities, prices, delivery terms, and a PO number the supplier references on their invoice. Without one, orders happen over email threads and phone calls, which is exactly how "I thought you approved 50 units" disputes start.

Here's what a proper purchase order needs, how it's different from an invoice, and how to generate one for free.

## What a purchase order actually does

A PO is a legal offer to buy, issued *before* the goods or services are delivered. Once the supplier accepts it (explicitly, or by fulfilling the order), it becomes a binding agreement on both sides. That's the core difference from an invoice — an invoice is issued *after* delivery to request payment; a PO is issued *before*, to authorize the order in the first place.

For a business, POs matter for three practical reasons:

- **A paper trail.** If pricing or quantity is ever disputed, the PO is the reference document both sides agreed to.
- **Budget control.** Requiring a PO before ordering forces a sign-off step, which stops surprise spending.
- **Matching on receipt.** Warehouses and accounts payable teams match incoming shipments and invoices against the original PO to catch errors before paying.

## What every purchase order needs

- **PO number** — unique, sequential, referenced by the supplier on their invoice.
- **Buyer and supplier details** — company name, address, contact.
- **Order date and requested delivery date.**
- **Line items** — SKU/item code, description, quantity, unit price, line total.
- **Shipping details** — delivery address and method.
- **Tax and totals** — subtotal, tax, shipping cost, discount, grand total.
- **Terms and conditions** — payment terms, delivery expectations, cancellation policy.
- **Authorized-by signature or name** — who approved the order on the buyer's side.

Missing any of these is where disputes creep in — a supplier who ships to the wrong address, or a mismatched unit price that shows up as a billing surprise weeks later.

## Purchase order vs. invoice vs. quote

These three documents get confused constantly because they cover the same transaction at different stages:

| Document | Issued by | When | Purpose |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Quote** | Supplier | Before the order | Proposed pricing, not yet binding |
| **Purchase order** | Buyer | After accepting the quote, before delivery | Formal authorization to proceed |
| **Invoice** | Supplier | After delivery | Request for payment |

A clean paper trail runs quote → PO → invoice, with the PO number carried through to the final invoice so accounts payable can match all three in seconds.

## Common mistakes on small-business POs

**No PO number, or a reused one.** Without a unique number, there's no reliable way to reference the order later, and a reused number makes matching invoices to POs error-prone.

**Vague line items.** "Supplies — $400" gives accounts payable nothing to match against the invoice. Use the same item descriptions and SKUs the supplier uses in their own catalog.

**Missing delivery terms.** Who pays shipping, what happens if goods arrive damaged, and the expected delivery window all belong on the PO — not left to an email exchange that's easy to lose track of.

**No sign-off trail.** Especially for larger orders, an authorized-by name protects both sides if the order is later questioned internally.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is a purchase order legally binding?**
Once a supplier accepts it — explicitly or by fulfilling the order — yes, it functions as a binding contract for the terms it specifies.

**Do small businesses really need formal POs?**
Even a one-person business benefits from a PO trail once order volume grows past a handful a month — it prevents disputes and makes bookkeeping dramatically easier at tax time.

**What's the difference between a PO and an invoice?**
A PO is issued by the buyer before delivery to authorize an order. An invoice is issued by the supplier after delivery to request payment. The PO number should appear on the invoice so both documents can be matched.

**Can I edit a purchase order after sending it?**
Once a supplier has accepted the original PO, changes should go through a formal PO amendment or a new PO — silently editing and resending the same PO number causes exactly the confusion a paper trail is meant to prevent.
