Frequently Asked Questions

Is there a pattern to what day people are born on?

Statistically, births are not evenly distributed across the week. In many countries, fewer babies are born on weekends because elective procedures such as scheduled C-sections and induced labors are less common then. Tuesday and Wednesday have historically been the most common birth days in the US, while Sunday tends to have the fewest births.

What is the Monday's child rhyme?

"Monday's child is fair of face, Tuesday's child is full of grace, Wednesday's child is full of woe, Thursday's child has far to go, Friday's child is loving and giving, Saturday's child works hard for a living, and the child born on the Sabbath day is bonny and blithe and good and gay." This well-known nursery rhyme dates to at least 1838 and has been a popular way to attach personality to birth days for generations.

Are there lucky or unlucky days to be born on?

Different cultures assign different meanings to birth days. In Western astrology, each day is linked to a planet and its associated qualities. In some East Asian traditions, the day of birth interacts with the birth year and month in systems like the Four Pillars of Destiny (Bazi). In Norse tradition, certain days were considered more auspicious than others. These are cultural beliefs rather than scientific facts, but many people enjoy exploring them.

How does the day of the week affect a birthday going forward?

Your birthday falls on the same day of the week roughly every 5–6 years in a regular pattern, because the Gregorian calendar repeats on a 400-year cycle. More practically, a birthday lands on a weekend (Saturday or Sunday) about 2 out of every 7 years. Monday's Child: Day-of-Week Folklore

For centuries, people have attached meaning to the day of the week a child was born on. The well-known "Monday's Child" nursery rhyme — dating to at least 1838 — assigns traits and fortunes to each day. Each day of the week is also associated with one of the classical seven "planets" (the Sun, Moon, Mars, Mercury, Jupiter, Venus, and Saturn), which gave the days their names in most European languages.
