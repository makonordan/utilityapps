Frequently Asked Questions

At what age can I retire in the US?

The full retirement age (FRA) for Social Security in the US is 67 for anyone born in 1960 or later. You can claim early benefits at 62, but your monthly payment will be permanently reduced by up to 30%. Waiting until 70 increases your benefit by 8% for each year beyond your FRA.

How do I calculate how many years until I can retire?

Subtract your current age from your target retirement age. For example, if you are 35 and want to retire at 65, you have 30 years remaining. This calculator does that math automatically and also shows you days, weekdays, weekends, and summers remaining — giving you a more visceral sense of the time ahead.

What age is considered early retirement?

In the US, any retirement before the full Social Security retirement age of 67 is technically early. However, the FIRE movement (Financial Independence, Retire Early) commonly targets retirement in the 40s or 50s. From a healthcare perspective, retiring before 65 means you are not yet eligible for Medicare, so private health insurance becomes a key planning factor.

How much money do I need to retire?

A widely used rule of thumb is the "25x rule" — save 25 times your expected annual retirement spending. This pairs with the "4% rule," which suggests you can safely withdraw 4% of your portfolio per year without running out of money over a 30-year retirement. For example, if you expect to spend $50,000 per year, you would need roughly $1.25 million saved.

What is the difference between retirement age and pension age?

Retirement age refers to when you personally choose to stop working, while pension age (or Social Security age) is when you become eligible to receive government benefits. These do not have to be the same. Many people retire before their pension age and live off savings or a private pension, then begin collecting Social Security later to maximize their monthly benefit.
