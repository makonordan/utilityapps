What Is BMI?

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a numerical value derived from a person's height and weight. It is calculated by dividing weight in kilograms by the square of height in metres (kg/m²) and is used worldwide as a screening tool to categorise individuals into weight status groups: underweight, normal weight, overweight, and obese. These categories correlate, in large population studies, with increased risk of certain diseases — although BMI is not a diagnostic tool on its own.

Metric:   BMI = weight (kg) ÷ height² (m)

Imperial: BMI = 703 × weight (lbs) ÷ height² (inches)

A Brief History

BMI was originally developed by the Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet in the 1830s as a way to statistically describe the "average man" in a population. He called it the Quetelet Index, and it was never intended for clinical use. In 1972, physiologist Ancel Keys coined the term "Body Mass Index" in the Journal of Chronic Diseases, proposing it for population-level health research. The WHO adopted standardised BMI cutoffs for global use in the 1990s, cementing its position as the world's most widely used weight screening metric.

Why BMI Is Widely Used

Despite its limitations, BMI remains the dominant weight screening tool in clinical and public health settings for three practical reasons. First, it requires only height and weight — measurable anywhere with basic equipment in seconds. Second, large cohort studies have consistently shown that higher BMI correlates with elevated risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, certain cancers, and all-cause mortality. Third, tracking BMI trends across populations provides an imperfect but accessible way to monitor the global trajectory of obesity.

Limitations of BMI

BMI is a proxy measure, not a direct measurement of body fat. Its most significant limitations:

  • •Muscle mass: Athletes and bodybuilders often register a high BMI due to muscle, not fat. BMI cannot distinguish between lean mass and fat mass — a fundamental weakness at the individual level.
  • •Fat distribution: BMI does not capture where fat is stored. Visceral fat (around internal organs) is metabolically more harmful than subcutaneous fat at the hips and thighs, even at the same BMI.
  • •Age: Muscle mass naturally declines and fat mass increases with age. Two people of the same age can have the same BMI but radically different body compositions, which the formula cannot detect.
  • •Ethnicity: People of Asian descent develop metabolic risk factors at lower BMI values than people of European descent — a difference large enough for the WHO to recommend separate cutoffs.
  • •Children: Adult BMI cutoffs do not apply to anyone under 20. Children's BMI must be interpreted using age- and sex-specific CDC percentile charts, not fixed thresholds.

BMI by Age Group

The same BMI number can mean different things depending on age, sex, and body composition.

🧑

Adults (Ages 20–64)

The standard WHO scale applies: 18.5–24.9 is Healthy Weight, 25.0–29.9 is Overweight, and ≥ 30.0 is Obese. This scale was developed primarily from studies of European adults and may not be equally accurate across all ethnic groups. For people of Asian descent, adjusted cutoffs of ≥ 23.0 (overweight) and ≥ 27.5 (obese) are recommended.

👴

Seniors (Ages 65+)

Research increasingly suggests that a BMI of 25–27 may be protective for adults over 65, providing metabolic reserve during illness or surgery — a phenomenon sometimes called the 'obesity paradox.' Muscle preservation through strength training matters more than a specific BMI target after 65. A high BMI still raises risk of joint problems, sleep apnoea, and certain cancers even in older adults.

👶

Children and Teens (Ages 2–19)

Adult BMI cutoffs do not apply to anyone under 20. For children and teens, BMI is assessed using age- and sex-specific percentile charts developed by the CDC. Underweight is below the 5th percentile; Healthy Weight is 5th–84th; Overweight is 85th–94th; Obese is at or above the 95th percentile for that age and sex.

Athletes and Highly Muscular Individuals

Athletes with significant muscle mass frequently register in the 'Overweight' or 'Obese' BMI range without carrying excess fat — muscle is denser than fat. For athletes, body fat percentage measured via DEXA scan, hydrostatic weighing, or skinfold calipers is a far more meaningful health indicator than BMI.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a normal BMI?

A normal (healthy) BMI for adults is between 18.5 and 24.9, according to the World Health Organization. This range is associated with the lowest risk of weight-related health problems such as cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and certain cancers. However, 'normal' varies by individual factors including muscle mass, bone density, age, and ethnicity.

Is BMI an accurate measure of health?

BMI is a useful population-level screening tool but has significant limitations as an individual health measure. It correlates moderately with body fat percentage but does not measure body fat directly. Athletes with high muscle mass may have a high BMI without excess fat. People with a 'normal' BMI can still have poor metabolic health, high visceral fat, or other risk factors. BMI should be interpreted alongside waist circumference, blood pressure, and blood markers.

What is a healthy BMI for women?

The WHO healthy BMI range of 18.5–24.9 applies to both men and women. However, women naturally carry more body fat at the same BMI — they require higher essential fat levels for hormonal function and reproduction. Women of Asian descent may face elevated health risks at BMI values that appear 'normal' by standard WHO cutoffs, and the adjusted thresholds (overweight ≥ 23.0, obese ≥ 27.5) are recommended for this population.

What is a healthy BMI for men?

For men, the WHO defines healthy weight as BMI 18.5–24.9. Men tend to carry less essential body fat than women, so a given BMI often represents less total fat in men. Men with high muscle mass — particularly athletes — frequently fall in the Overweight range despite being metabolically healthy. Body fat percentage and waist circumference are more informative for muscular individuals.

What is a healthy BMI for seniors?

For adults over 65, some research suggests that a slightly higher BMI of 25–27 may be protective, offering metabolic reserve during illness or surgery — a phenomenon sometimes called the "obesity paradox." However, a high BMI still raises risk of joint problems and certain diseases in older adults. Preserving muscle mass through resistance training matters more than achieving a specific BMI number after age 65.

How do I calculate BMI manually?

In metric units: BMI = weight (kg) ÷ height² (m). For example, a person who weighs 70 kg and is 1.75 m tall has BMI = 70 ÷ (1.75 × 1.75) = 22.9. In imperial units: BMI = 703 × weight (lbs) ÷ height² (inches). For a 154 lb person who is 5′9″ (69 inches): 703 × 154 ÷ (69 × 69) = 22.7.

What BMI is considered obese?

The WHO classifies obesity as BMI ≥ 30.0. There are three classes: Class I (30.0–34.9) carries high risk; Class II (35.0–39.9) carries very high risk; Class III (≥ 40.0), sometimes called severe or morbid obesity, carries extremely high risk. For people of Asian descent, obesity may be defined at BMI ≥ 27.5 due to differences in body composition and associated risk profiles.

Can you have a normal BMI but still be unhealthy?

Yes. A person in the healthy BMI range can still be metabolically unhealthy if they carry a high proportion of visceral fat (fat around internal organs) despite having a low overall weight — sometimes called "normal-weight obesity" or being "skinny fat." Blood markers like fasting glucose, triglycerides, HDL cholesterol, and blood pressure are better predictors of metabolic health than BMI alone.

What is the BMI formula?

The standard formula in metric units is: BMI = weight (kg) ÷ height² (m). In imperial units: BMI = 703 × weight (lbs) ÷ height² (inches). The same formula applies to all adults regardless of age or sex — one of its key limitations, since body composition changes significantly with age and differs between sexes.

How often should I check my BMI?

Once or twice per year is sufficient for most adults. More frequent monitoring may be appropriate if you are actively managing weight or working with a healthcare provider on a weight-related condition. BMI is most useful as a long-term trend indicator — day-to-day weight fluctuations from hydration and food intake can shift BMI by 1–2 points without reflecting any real change in body fat.

What is a good BMI for my age?

For adults aged 20–64, the WHO standard range of 18.5–24.9 applies. For adults over 65, evidence supports a slightly higher target of 22–27. For children and teens under 20, adult BMI cutoffs do not apply — weight status must be assessed using age- and sex-specific CDC percentile charts. A BMI below the 5th percentile is underweight; above the 95th percentile is obese for the child's age and sex.

Does BMI differ by ethnicity?

Yes, significantly. The WHO recommends lower BMI cutoffs for people of Asian descent because studies show they develop metabolic risk factors — type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease — at lower BMI values than people of European descent. For Asian populations, the WHO recommends considering overweight at BMI ≥ 23.0 and obesity at BMI ≥ 27.5, compared to the standard thresholds of 25.0 and 30.0.

What is the difference between BMI and body fat percentage?

BMI is calculated from height and weight only. Body fat percentage (BF%) is the proportion of body weight made up of fat tissue, requiring measurement via DEXA scan, skinfold calipers, hydrostatic weighing, or validated estimation formulas. BMI correlates roughly with BF% at the population level but fails at the individual level — two people with the same BMI can differ in body fat by 10% or more. For athletes, BF% is far more meaningful than BMI.

What should I do if my BMI is high?

A high BMI is a signal, not a diagnosis. First, assess your overall metabolic health: blood pressure, fasting blood glucose, cholesterol, and waist circumference provide a more complete picture. If your BMI is above 30 (or 27.5 for Asian populations), discuss it with a healthcare provider. Sustainable weight management relies on regular physical activity (≥ 150 minutes of moderate exercise per week), a diet lower in ultra-processed foods and added sugar, and sufficient sleep (7–9 hours). Avoid rapid weight-loss approaches, which typically cause muscle loss and are rarely maintained long-term.
