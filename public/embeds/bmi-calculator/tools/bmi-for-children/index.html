Frequently Asked Questions

How is BMI interpreted differently for children than adults?

Adult BMI uses fixed cutoffs (e.g., BMI 25 = Overweight) regardless of age or sex. For children and teens, the same BMI number means very different things depending on the child's age and sex, because children's bodies change rapidly as they grow. Instead of fixed cutoffs, pediatricians use BMI-for-age percentile charts — comparing the child's BMI to other children of the same age and sex from a national reference population.

What BMI percentile is considered healthy for a child?

The CDC defines Healthy Weight as a BMI between the 5th and 84th percentiles for age and sex. Underweight is below the 5th percentile, Overweight is 85th–94th percentile, and Obese is at or above the 95th percentile. These cutoffs are based on national growth data and are consistent across different body types more reliably than adult BMI cutoffs.

My child is above the 95th percentile — should I be worried?

A BMI at or above the 95th percentile is defined as Obese by the CDC and warrants discussion with your child's pediatrician, but it should not cause panic. BMI is a screening tool, not a diagnosis. A pediatrician will assess the child's overall health, growth trajectory, diet, activity levels, and family history before drawing any conclusions. A single BMI measurement doesn't tell the whole story.

Why does a child's BMI change as they age?

Children's bodies change composition throughout growth — they gain bone, muscle, and fat at different rates depending on age and sex. BMI naturally drops from ages 1–5 (a phase called adiposity rebound), then rises through puberty as body fat increases. Boys and girls follow different growth curves, which is why sex-specific percentile charts are essential. This is why a 'normal' adult BMI cutoff of 25 is irrelevant for a 7-year-old.

How accurate is BMI for assessing a child's health?

BMI-for-age is a useful population-level screening tool, but it has limitations at the individual level. Athletic children with high muscle mass may have elevated BMI without excess fat. Children from certain ethnic backgrounds may have different body fat distributions at the same BMI. BMI also does not capture where fat is stored — abdominal fat carries higher health risk than fat stored elsewhere. Pediatricians use BMI alongside other measures for a complete picture.

What should I do if my child's BMI is outside the healthy range?

The most important step is to discuss the result with your child's pediatrician — not to make dietary or lifestyle changes based solely on an online calculator. If your pediatrician confirms a concern, they may recommend working with a registered dietitian, increasing physical activity, or monitoring growth over time. It is important not to put young children on restrictive diets without medical supervision, as this can interfere with normal growth and development.
