Frequently Asked Questions

What is body fat percentage?

Body fat percentage is the proportion of your total body weight made up of fat tissue. It includes essential fat (needed for survival) and storage fat (accumulated in adipose tissue). Unlike BMI, it distinguishes fat mass from lean mass — muscle, bone, water, and organs.

What is a healthy body fat percentage?

Healthy ranges differ by sex. For men: 6–13% (athletes), 14–17% (fitness), 18–24% (average), 25%+ (obese). For women: 14–20% (athletes), 21–24% (fitness), 25–31% (average), 32%+ (obese). Women naturally carry more essential fat due to hormonal and reproductive requirements.

How accurate is the Deurenberg formula?

The Deurenberg formula estimates body fat from BMI, age, and sex with a margin of error of roughly ±3–5% in most adults. It is validated in large population studies and is a reasonable screening tool, but individual variation — especially high muscle mass — limits precision. Athletes will have their body fat systematically overestimated.

What are the most accurate ways to measure body fat?

In order of accuracy: DEXA (dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry) scan is the gold standard. Hydrostatic weighing measures body density precisely. Air displacement plethysmography (Bod Pod) is fast and accurate. Skinfold calipers by a trained technician are practical and widely available. Bioelectrical impedance (BIA scales) is convenient but sensitive to hydration status.

Does body fat percentage change with age?

Yes. As adults age, muscle mass naturally decreases (sarcopenia) while fat mass tends to increase — even without weight gain. Men typically gain body fat from their 30s; women often see a larger shift around menopause. The Deurenberg formula accounts for this by including age as a variable, which is why two people with the same BMI but different ages will receive different estimates.

What's the difference between essential fat and storage fat?

Essential fat is the minimum required for basic physiological functions: protecting organs, regulating hormones, supporting nerve function, and enabling reproduction. Men need at least 2–5%; women need 10–13% (higher due to sex-specific fat in breasts and reproductive organs). Storage fat is surplus accumulated in adipose tissue as an energy reserve — this is the fat most people aim to reduce.
