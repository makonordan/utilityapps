Frequently Asked Questions

What is TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure)?

TDEE is the total number of calories your body burns in a day, including all physical activity. It combines your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) with an activity multiplier. Eating at your TDEE maintains your current weight — eating below it creates a deficit for fat loss.

What is BMR and how is it calculated?

BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) is the number of calories your body burns at complete rest to maintain basic functions like breathing, circulation, and cell repair. This calculator uses the Mifflin-St Jeor equation — the most validated formula for most adults. For men: BMR = (10 × kg) + (6.25 × cm) − (5 × age) + 5. For women: same formula but − 161 instead of + 5.

How many calories do I need to lose weight?

To lose weight, you need to consume fewer calories than your TDEE. A deficit of 500 calories per day produces roughly 0.5 kg (1 lb) of fat loss per week. A 1,000-calorie deficit targets approximately 1 kg per week. Deficits beyond 1,000 calories/day are not recommended without medical supervision as they risk muscle loss and nutrient deficiencies.

How many calories to lose 1 kg per week?

One kilogram of body fat contains approximately 7,700 calories. To lose 1 kg per week you need a daily deficit of roughly 1,100 calories (7,700 ÷ 7). This is aggressive — a 500-calorie deficit losing 0.5 kg per week is far more sustainable, preserves muscle, and is much easier to maintain long-term without rebound.

What is a calorie deficit and how do I create one?

A calorie deficit means consuming fewer calories than you burn (TDEE). You can create one by eating less, moving more, or combining both. A moderate deficit of 300–500 calories per day is ideal for most people — large enough to produce visible results without triggering excessive hunger, fatigue, or muscle loss.

How accurate are online calorie calculators?

Calorie calculators based on the Mifflin-St Jeor equation are accurate within 10–15% for most people. Individual variation in metabolism, lean muscle mass, hormone levels, gut microbiome, and medication can shift actual needs. Use the result as a starting point, then track your weight over 2–3 weeks and adjust by ±100–200 calories based on real-world outcomes.

What are macronutrients and why do they matter?

Macronutrients — protein, carbohydrates, and fat — are the three main sources of calories. Protein (4 kcal/g) supports muscle repair, immune function, and satiety. Carbohydrates (4 kcal/g) fuel exercise, brain function, and mood. Fat (9 kcal/g) supports hormones, joint health, and absorption of fat-soluble vitamins. Balancing your macros — not just total calories — improves body composition, energy, and long-term adherence.

How many calories should I eat to gain weight or muscle?

To gain weight, eat above your TDEE. A surplus of 300–500 calories per day supports lean muscle gain when paired with progressive resistance training. Larger surpluses tend to produce more fat gain than muscle. Aim for 1.6–2.2 g of protein per kg of bodyweight to maximise muscle protein synthesis during a bulk.
