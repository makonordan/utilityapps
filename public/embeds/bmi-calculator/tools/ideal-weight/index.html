Frequently Asked Questions

What is ideal body weight and why does it matter?

Ideal body weight (IBW) estimates the healthiest weight for a given height and sex. It's used in clinical settings to calculate drug dosages, assess nutritional status, and guide weight management. IBW is a rough guide — not a target to chase — as health depends on many factors beyond weight alone.

Which ideal weight formula is most accurate?

No single formula is definitively most accurate for everyone. Devine is the most widely used clinically. Robinson and Miller were developed to correct perceived biases in Devine. Hamwi is common in pharmacy. All four have similar accuracy at average heights but diverge at extremes. The average of all four provides the most balanced estimate.

Does ideal weight account for muscle mass and body composition?

No. All four formulas are based solely on height and sex. They do not account for muscle mass, bone density, ethnicity, or fitness level. A heavily muscled athlete and a sedentary person of the same height will get identical results, even though their optimal weights are likely very different. Use IBW as a starting point, not a precise target.

Is BMI-based healthy weight better than formula-based ideal weight?

Both have limitations. BMI range (18.5–24.9) gives a range rather than a single number, which is more realistic. IBW formulas give a single value, making them useful for clinical calculations. For general guidance, the BMI healthy range is more widely validated and accounts for a broader spectrum of body weights.

How do I know if I am at my ideal weight?

Rather than one number, look at several indicators: BMI (18.5–24.9), waist circumference (below 94 cm for men, 80 cm for women), energy levels, fitness ability, and blood markers like blood pressure and cholesterol. Weight alone is a poor measure of health — your doctor is the best resource for a complete picture.

Does ideal weight change with age?

The formulas don't adjust for age, but body composition changes as we get older. Research suggests that slightly higher BMI (up to ~27) may be beneficial for adults over 65, as some body fat provides reserve during illness. For older adults, maintaining muscle mass through resistance exercise matters more than achieving a specific scale number.
