Frequently Asked Questions

What is the waist-to-hip ratio?

The waist-to-hip ratio (WHR) is your waist circumference divided by your hip circumference. It measures how fat is distributed across your body — specifically whether you carry more fat around the abdomen (central obesity) or around the hips and thighs (peripheral fat storage). The WHO uses WHR as an independent predictor of cardiovascular disease and type 2 diabetes risk.

What is a healthy waist-to-hip ratio?

For men, a ratio below 0.90 is considered low risk; 0.90–0.99 is moderate risk; 1.00 or above is high risk. For women, below 0.80 is low risk; 0.80–0.89 is moderate; 0.90 or above is high risk. These thresholds come from the World Health Organization's 2011 guidelines.

Why does the waist-to-hip ratio matter for cardiovascular health?

Abdominal (visceral) fat — fat stored around internal organs — is metabolically active and inflammatory. It raises triglycerides, lowers HDL cholesterol, promotes insulin resistance, and elevates blood pressure. People with a high WHR (apple shape) have significantly elevated risk of heart attack, stroke, and type 2 diabetes even when their overall body weight is in a healthy range. WHR captures this risk better than BMI alone.

What is the difference between apple and pear body shapes?

Apple-shaped bodies carry more fat around the waist and abdomen — a pattern linked to visceral (organ) fat and higher metabolic risk. Pear-shaped bodies store fat preferentially around the hips, buttocks, and thighs — subcutaneous fat that is metabolically less harmful. Research consistently shows that apple-shaped individuals face higher rates of cardiovascular disease, even at the same total body weight.

How do I measure my waist and hips correctly?

Waist: Stand relaxed (not sucking in), measure at the narrowest point of your torso, typically 2–3 cm above the navel. Use a flexible tape measure held parallel to the floor. Hips: Measure at the widest point of your buttocks, with feet together. Take both measurements without clothing and repeat twice to confirm. Morning measurements before eating are most consistent.

Can exercise and diet improve my waist-to-hip ratio?

Yes. Aerobic exercise (particularly vigorous cardio) is the most effective way to reduce visceral abdominal fat, which lowers your waist measurement and improves WHR. Strength training preserves muscle, which improves body composition. A diet lower in refined carbohydrates, added sugar, and alcohol preferentially reduces abdominal fat. Hip measurement tends to be more stable, so waist reduction is the main lever.
