Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about browser-based image compression.

Is this image compressor really free?

Yes, completely free — no account, no subscription, no hidden limits. The tool is supported by ads that keep it free for everyone.

Are my images uploaded to your server?

No. All compression happens inside your browser using the Canvas API and Web Workers. Your images never leave your device at any point.

What image formats are supported?

The compressor supports JPEG (JPG), PNG, and WebP as both input and output. You can also convert between formats — for example, JPEG input with WebP output.

How much can I compress an image?

Typical savings range from 40% to 90% depending on the original image and quality setting. A 4 MB photo can often be reduced to under 500 KB with no visible quality difference.

Will compression make my image blurry?

At quality settings of 70% and above, compression artifacts are invisible to most viewers. The before/after slider lets you see the difference before downloading.

What's the difference between lossy and lossless compression?

Lossy compression (used by JPEG and WebP) permanently removes some image data to achieve smaller files. Lossless compression (PNG) reduces file size without removing data, but achieves smaller savings. For photos, lossy is almost always the right choice.

How do I compress images for a website?

Upload your image, set quality to 75–85%, choose WebP output, and download. For JPEG photos aim for under 200 KB. For full-width hero images, stay under 500 KB.

What image format is best for web?

WebP is the best format for the web — it is 25–34% smaller than JPEG at equivalent quality, supports transparency like PNG, and is supported by 95%+ of browsers.

Can I compress multiple images at once?

Yes — use the Bulk Image Compressor tool to upload up to 20 images, compress them all in one click, and download as a ZIP file.

How do I compress images for email?

Most email clients flag images over 1 MB and some clients block them entirely. Aim for under 500 KB per image. Use JPEG or WebP and set quality to around 70–80%.
