Who Needs Bulk Image Compression?

Any workflow that involves optimizing more than a handful of images benefits from batch processing.

eCommerce product launches

Uploading 50 product photos to Shopify or WooCommerce uncompressed will slow your store pages and hurt conversions. Bulk compress the entire shoot before upload.

Blog and content migration

Moving an existing site or uploading a backlog of articles? Run all the images through the bulk compressor first — don't optimize them one by one.

Real estate listings

Property photos from a DSLR are often 6–12 MB each. Bulk compress to under 500 KB before adding to your listing portal — pages load faster and clients see more photos.

Event and portfolio galleries

Photographers delivering edited sets to clients benefit enormously from batch compression. Compress the full gallery before zipping and sending.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about bulk image compression.

How many images can I compress at once?

The bulk compressor accepts up to 20 images per session. If you have more, compress in batches — download the first ZIP, clear the list, then add the next batch.

Why does it process one at a time instead of all at once?

Processing images in parallel would launch multiple web workers simultaneously, potentially consuming several gigabytes of RAM on large images. Sequential processing keeps the browser stable and responsive regardless of how many files you add.

Can I mix different formats (JPG and PNG) in the same batch?

Yes. You can add any combination of JPG, PNG, and WebP files to the queue. The output format selector applies to all files in the batch — if you choose WebP output, every image in the batch is converted to WebP.

Are my images uploaded anywhere when using bulk compression?

No. Like all tools on this site, bulk compression runs entirely in your browser. None of your files are transmitted to any server. The ZIP file is assembled locally in your browser before download.

What happens if one image fails to compress?

Failed files are marked with an "Error" status in the table. The rest of the batch continues processing. After the batch completes, you can retry failed files by clicking "Compress All" again — it only re-processes files that are still in a waiting or error state.
