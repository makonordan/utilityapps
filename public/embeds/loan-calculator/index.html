How Mortgage Payments Actually Work

What is amortization?

Amortization is the process of paying down a loan with equal monthly payments. The magic — and the trick — is that even though your monthly payment is the same every month, the split between interest and principal changes dramatically over time. On a $300,000 mortgage at 6.5% over 30 years, your fixed monthly payment is about $1,896. In month one, $1,625 of that goes to interest and only $271 actually reduces your loan balance. By the final payment, the proportions are reversed. Same payment, very different work.

Why early payments are mostly interest

Interest is always charged on the current outstanding balance. In month one of a 30-year loan, that balance is the entire principal, so the interest hit is largest. As you chip away at the balance, less interest accrues each month, freeing up more of your fixed payment to attack principal. This front-loaded interest pattern is why selling or refinancing early in the loan feels punishing — you’ve paid years of mostly interest and have very little equity to show. It’s also why making extra principal payments early in the loan has outsized lifetime value.

How extra payments dramatically reduce total interest

Every extra dollar paid toward principal removes that dollar from every future interest calculation. Send an extra $100/month on the $300k mortgage above, and the loan pays off about 4 years early — saving roughly $50,000 in interest. Send $200 extra and you save closer to $80,000. The math compounds because every reduction in balance reduces all subsequent interest charges. The best returns come from extra payments in the first 10 years, when the balance is largest. Even one extra payment per year (the “13th payment” trick) shaves about 4 years off a 30-year loan.

Fixed rate vs adjustable rate

A fixed-rate mortgage locks your interest rate for the entire term. Your principal-and-interest payment never changes — predictable but rate tradeoffs. An adjustable-rate mortgage (ARM) starts with a lower fixed rate for an introductory period (5/1 ARMs are fixed for 5 years, then adjust annually) and then floats with a benchmark rate. ARMs make sense when (1) you’re confident you’ll move or refinance before the adjustment period, or (2) rates are falling. The risk: if rates rise, your monthly payment can climb sharply once the fixed period ends. After 2022’s rate spike, a lot of ARM holders got squeezed.

What affects your interest rate

Lenders set rates based on their risk — and your inputs into that risk equation. The biggest levers, ranked:

  • Credit score: a 760+ FICO can save 0.5–1% over a 680 FICO. On a $300k loan, that gap is roughly $80,000 in lifetime interest.
  • Loan-to-value (LTV) ratio: down payments below 20% trigger PMI and tighter underwriting. 20%+ unlocks better rates.
  • Loan type: 15-year rates are typically 0.5% lower than 30-year; conforming loans are cheaper than jumbo; government-backed (FHA/VA) have their own rate tiers.
  • Debt-to-income ratio: high existing debt loads can bump you to a worse rate tier or fail you out of best-rate eligibility entirely.
  • Property type: primary residences get the best rates; second homes pay ~0.25% more; investment properties pay 0.5–0.75% more.

Frequently Asked Questions

16 of the most common questions on mortgages, loans, and personal finance.

How is a mortgage payment calculated?▾

Standard amortization formula: M = P × [r(1+r)^n] ÷ [(1+r)^n − 1], where P is the loan amount, r is the monthly rate (annual ÷ 12), and n is the number of monthly payments. The result is your fixed P&I (principal + interest). Your full monthly housing cost (PITI) adds property tax, homeowner insurance, and PMI on top.

What is amortization?▾

Amortization is the process of paying down a loan with equal periodic payments. Each payment is split between interest (calculated on the current balance) and principal (the part that reduces what you owe). Early in the loan, most of each payment is interest because the balance is large. As the balance shrinks, the interest portion shrinks and more goes to principal — until the final payment is almost entirely principal.

How much down payment do I need for a mortgage?▾

Conventional loans accept as little as 3% down for first-time buyers (5% otherwise). FHA requires 3.5% with a 580+ credit score. VA and USDA loans allow 0% down for qualifying borrowers. The 20% threshold matters because below it you pay PMI (private mortgage insurance), typically 0.3–1.5% of the loan annually.

What credit score do I need for a mortgage?▾

Conventional loans usually require 620+. FHA loans accept 580+ with 3.5% down (or 500+ with 10% down). VA loans don’t set a strict floor but most lenders want 580–620+. The best rates start at 740 and improve again at 760+. Below 680, the rate premium can add tens of thousands over a 30-year loan.

Should I get a 15-year or 30-year mortgage?▾

15-year mortgages have rates ~0.5–0.75% lower, build equity ~3× faster, and save enormous amounts in total interest. The downside: monthly payments are roughly 50% higher. Choose 30 years if you need cash-flow flexibility (and consider extra principal payments). Choose 15 years if the higher payment is comfortable and you want guaranteed forced savings.

How do extra payments affect my mortgage?▾

Even small extras have outsized impact because the saved interest compounds. On a 30-year $300k mortgage at 6.5%, paying $100 extra/month shaves about 4 years off the term and saves roughly $50,000 in interest. The earlier in the loan you start, the bigger the impact, since you’re killing balance during the high-interest phase.

What is PMI and when can I remove it?▾

Private Mortgage Insurance protects the lender (not you) when your down payment is below 20%. It typically costs 0.3–1.5% of the loan amount annually, paid monthly. Federal law (HPA) requires lenders to automatically cancel PMI when your loan balance drops to 78% of the original home value, and you can request cancellation at 80%. FHA loans have a different program (MIP) that often runs the full life of the loan.

What does my monthly payment include (PITI)?▾

PITI = Principal + Interest + Taxes + Insurance. Principal is the part that reduces your loan balance. Interest is the lender’s cost of capital. Taxes are the local property tax (typically 0.5–2.5% of home value annually, divided by 12). Insurance is homeowners insurance, often required by the lender. PMI and HOA fees are sometimes added on top.

What is debt-to-income ratio?▾

DTI is the percentage of your gross monthly income that goes to debt payments. Front-end DTI counts only housing (PITI); lenders generally cap it at 28%. Back-end DTI counts all monthly debt payments combined; conventional loans cap it at 43%, FHA up to 50% with strong reserves. Lower DTI = better rates and more buying power.

How much house can I afford?▾

A common rule: home price ≤ 4–5× your gross annual income, and monthly housing payment ≤ 28% of gross monthly income. The exact number depends on your interest rate, term, down payment, existing debts, and local property taxes. Use the affordability calculator on this site for a personalized estimate.

What is the difference between interest rate and APR?▾

The interest rate is the cost of borrowing the principal. APR (Annual Percentage Rate) wraps in upfront fees — origination, discount points, certain closing costs — and re-expresses everything as a single annualized rate. APR is the better number for comparing offers across lenders. Federal law (TILA) requires lenders to disclose APR alongside the interest rate.

Is it better to make a large down payment or invest the difference?▾

Compare the mortgage rate to your expected investment return after taxes. If your mortgage is 6.5% and your investment portfolio reliably returns 7%+ after taxes, investing wins long-term. If returns are uncertain or you’re close to retirement, a larger down payment is the safer, guaranteed “return” via interest avoided. Most planners suggest splitting: at least 20% down (avoids PMI), invest beyond that.

When should I refinance my mortgage?▾

The traditional rule of a 1% rate drop is rough. The real test is the break-even point: closing costs ÷ monthly savings. If you’ll keep the loan past break-even, refinancing pays off; if you’re selling or moving sooner, you lose money. Use the refinance calculator on this site for a precise break-even calculation.

What is the avalanche vs snowball debt payoff method?▾

Avalanche pays off your highest-interest-rate debt first while making minimums on the rest — mathematically optimal, saves the most interest. Snowball pays off your smallest balance first regardless of rate — slower in dollar terms, but the early wins build motivation. Both work; the best plan is the one you’ll stick with.

How does compound interest work?▾

Compound interest is interest earned on both the original principal and on previously accumulated interest. Each compounding period (daily, monthly, annually), interest is added to the balance — and the next period’s interest is calculated on that larger balance. Over decades, this turns small monthly contributions into very large balances. The Rule of 72 is a quick approximation: divide 72 by the rate to estimate years to double.

Can I pay off my mortgage early?▾

Almost always yes. Most U.S. mortgages have no prepayment penalty (a few subprime products did historically). You can pay extra principal monthly, make a single lump-sum payment, or do a "13th-payment-per-year" strategy (one extra payment annually, which trims a 30-year mortgage to about 26 years). Confirm your loan terms allow it before sending extras.
