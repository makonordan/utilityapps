Understanding DTI Ratios

Lenders look at two DTI numbers — front-end and back-end. Both are calculated against your gross (pre-tax) monthly income.

  • Front-end DTI: housing costs (P&I + taxes + insurance + HOA) divided by gross monthly income. Most conventional lenders prefer this below 28%.
  • Back-end DTI: all monthly debt obligations (housing + auto + student loans + credit card minimums + alimony, etc.) divided by gross monthly income. Conventional limit is typically 43%; FHA can stretch to 50% with strong reserves and credit.

Reducing existing debts is often the fastest way to lift your affordability ceiling — every $100/month of revolving debt eliminated translates to roughly $20,000 in additional loan capacity at current rates.

Down Payment Minimums

  • Conventional loan: as little as 3% down for first-time buyers (5% otherwise). Below 20% triggers PMI of ~0.3–1.5% annually.
  • FHA loan: 3.5% down with a 580+ credit score. Carries upfront and monthly mortgage insurance for the life of the loan in most cases.
  • VA loan: 0% down for qualifying veterans, active-duty, and surviving spouses. No PMI ever. Funding fee applies.
  • USDA loan: 0% down in eligible rural areas with income limits.
  • Jumbo loan (above conforming limits): typically 10–20% down with stricter DTI and reserves.

The Pre-Approval Process

Before making offers, get pre-approved by 2–3 lenders. The process takes 1–3 business days and requires:

  • 2 most recent pay stubs (or 2 years of tax returns if self-employed)
  • 2 months of bank and investment statements
  • 2 years of W-2s
  • Photo ID and SSN for credit pull
  • Documentation of any other income (alimony, rental, bonus history)

Pre-approval pulls your credit (a hard inquiry, ~5 point dip), but multiple mortgage inquiries within a 14–45 day window count as a single inquiry under FICO scoring — so shop rates aggressively in that window.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much house can I afford on my salary?▾

A common rule of thumb: home price ≤ 4–5× gross annual income, with a monthly housing payment ≤ 28% of gross monthly income. The right number depends on your interest rate, term, down payment, existing debts, and local property taxes — which is exactly what this calculator estimates.

What is the 28/36 rule?▾

It’s the most-used affordability guideline in U.S. mortgage lending. The “28” is the front-end DTI limit: housing costs (P&I + taxes + insurance + HOA) shouldn’t exceed 28% of gross monthly income. The “36” is the back-end DTI limit: all monthly debt payments (housing + everything else) shouldn’t exceed 36%. Many lenders now stretch back-end up to 43% or 50% for borrowers with strong compensating factors.

What’s a good DTI ratio for a mortgage?▾

Below 36% back-end is widely considered healthy. 36–43% is acceptable to most lenders. Above 43% triggers tighter scrutiny and may require an FHA loan or significant compensating factors (large reserves, high credit score, large down payment).

How does my down payment affect what I can afford?▾

A larger down payment lowers the loan amount (so you qualify for a higher purchase price for the same monthly payment), eliminates PMI once you cross 20%, and signals reserves to lenders. The flip side: putting too much down can deplete cash reserves needed for closing costs, moving, and unexpected repairs.

Should I get pre-approved before house hunting?▾

Yes. Pre-approval involves the lender pulling your credit and verifying income/assets — it produces a real loan estimate. Sellers in competitive markets typically won’t take an offer seriously without one. Pre-approval letters are usually valid for 60–90 days.

Does my credit score affect how much I can afford?▾

Strongly. The same loan amount can cost 1–2% more in interest with a lower credit score, which translates to ~10–20% less house for the same monthly payment. Most conventional loans require 620+; the best rates start at 740 and improve again at 760+.

What’s included in PITI?▾

PITI is the four-part monthly housing cost lenders evaluate: Principal, Interest, property Taxes, and homeowner Insurance. PMI and HOA fees are sometimes added on top when calculating front-end DTI.

How much should I save for closing costs?▾

Closing costs typically run 2–5% of the loan amount in the U.S. — that’s on top of your down payment. On a $400,000 home with 20% down ($80,000), expect another $6,400–$16,000 at closing. Some loan programs roll closing costs into the loan; sellers can also contribute via concessions.

What’s the difference between pre-qualified and pre-approved?▾

Pre-qualified is a quick, often-self-reported estimate of what you might borrow. Pre-approved involves a credit pull and document verification — it carries weight with sellers. Always go for pre-approval before submitting offers.

Should I borrow the maximum I qualify for?▾

Almost never. Lender caps assume you’ll happily eat ramen for 30 years. The “Conservative” scenario in this calculator (25% front-end DTI) leaves room for retirement contributions, emergencies, kids, travel, and rate-up risk on adjustable loans. Most financial advisors suggest spending 20–25% of gross income on housing if you can.
