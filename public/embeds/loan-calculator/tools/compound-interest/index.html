The Compound Interest Formula

The mathematical formula for compound interest with periodic contributions:

A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt) + PMT × [((1 + r/n)^(nt) − 1) ÷ (r/n)]
  • A = final amount
  • P = initial principal
  • r = annual rate (decimal)
  • n = compounding periods per year
  • t = years
  • PMT = periodic contribution

The first half of the formula compounds the principal; the second half is the future value of an annuity (your recurring contributions). This calculator handles the math behind the scenes.

Why Time Matters Most

Two friends both invest $200/month at 7%. One starts at 25 and stops at 35. The other starts at 35 and never stops. Who has more at 65?

  • Early-starter (10 years of contributions, then 30 years of pure compounding): ~$294,000
  • Late-starter (30 straight years of contributions): ~$245,000

The early-starter contributed three times less ($24,000 vs $72,000) and still came out ahead. That gap is compounding doing its quietest, most powerful work — and why starting today, even with small amounts, beats starting "later" with bigger amounts.

Numbers are illustrative — they assume a constant 7% annual return.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is compound interest?▾

Compound interest is interest calculated not just on your original principal, but also on all the interest you’ve already earned. Each compounding period, your balance grows — and the next period’s interest is calculated on that larger balance. Over long timeframes the curve becomes exponential, which is what people mean by "the magic of compounding".

What does compounding frequency change?▾

It controls how often interest is added to your balance. Daily compounding (365×/yr) earns slightly more than monthly (12×/yr), which earns more than annual (1×/yr). The differences are smaller than most people think — at 7% annual rate over 30 years, daily vs annual compounding differs by only ~3%. Frequency matters most at very high rates and very long horizons.

Is the result guaranteed?▾

No. This calculator assumes a constant annual return. Real-world investments — stocks, bonds, real estate — fluctuate year to year. The S&P 500 has averaged ~10% annually since 1957 but has had several years of −20% or worse, and several years of +30%. Use this calculator for planning, not for guarantees.

What rate should I use for retirement planning?▾

A conservative starting point is 6–7% real return for diversified stock-heavy portfolios over multi-decade timeframes (after subtracting ~3% for inflation). For bonds-heavy portfolios, use 2–4%. For high-yield savings or CDs, use the current quoted APY. Always model both an optimistic and a pessimistic rate to bracket your actual outcome.

Should I prioritize a bigger initial deposit or larger monthly contributions?▾

Time on each dollar matters more than when it arrived. A $10,000 lump sum at age 25 grows to ~$108,000 by 65 at 7%. The same total ($10,000) added in $250/month chunks over the same 40 years grows to less, because dollars added in year 30 only have 10 years to compound. That said, monthly contributions are usually how real money flows in — the lesson is to start now rather than waiting to "have a lump sum".

What is the Rule of 72?▾

Divide 72 by your annual rate to estimate how many years it takes to double your money. At 6% your money doubles roughly every 12 years; at 9% every 8 years; at 12% every 6 years. The rule is approximate — it’s most accurate between 4% and 15%. For very high or low rates, run the actual numbers.

How does inflation affect these numbers?▾

This calculator shows nominal returns — actual dollars at maturity. Inflation erodes purchasing power: $1 million in 30 years at 3% inflation has the buying power of ~$412,000 today. To plan in today’s dollars, subtract your expected inflation rate (historically ~3%) from the return rate before plugging it in. So a 7% nominal return becomes ~4% real.

Where can I actually earn these rates?▾

High-yield savings accounts: 4–5% APY (rates vary with the Fed). Certificates of Deposit (CDs): 4–5% locked for the term. Bonds and bond ETFs: 4–6%. Diversified index funds (e.g. S&P 500): historically ~10% nominal, ~7% real, with year-to-year volatility. Higher-risk individual stocks or sector funds: variable, no guarantees. Treasury I Bonds: tied to inflation. Match your time horizon to risk: short horizons → high-yield savings; long horizons (10+ years) → diversified index funds.
