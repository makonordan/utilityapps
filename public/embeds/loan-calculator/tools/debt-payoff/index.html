🏔 The Avalanche Method

How it works: List every debt by interest rate, highest to lowest. Pay the minimum on all of them. Put every extra dollar toward the top of the list. When that debt is gone, the entire payment (minimum + extra) rolls down to the next.

Why it wins on math: Interest accrues on balance × rate. The highest-rate debt is bleeding you fastest. Killing it first stops the bleed quickest.

Best for: People who are analytically motivated, can stay consistent without frequent wins, and have at least one high-rate debt (credit cards, payday loans, store cards).

Watch out for: If your highest-rate debt is also your largest, you may go many months without seeing a debt fully eliminated. Some people lose motivation and quit.

⛄ The Snowball Method

How it works: List every debt by balance, smallest to largest. Pay the minimum on all of them. Put every extra dollar toward the smallest balance. When it’s gone, roll that payment into the next smallest.

Why it wins on behavior: Each eliminated debt is a tangible win. Wins build momentum. Momentum beats math.

Best for: People who have struggled to follow through on financial plans before, have several small balances, or are motivated by visible progress.

Watch out for: You will pay more in total interest. If you have one very large, very-high-rate debt (e.g. $20k credit card at 24%) and several small cheap ones, the cost of Snowball can be hundreds or thousands.

The Key Concept: Rolling Payments

Both methods rely on the same trick: when a debt is paid off, you don’t pocket the freed-up payment — you redirect it to the next target. Your total monthly debt budget stays constant from start to finish.

Example: you owe $300 minimum across three debts and pay $100 extra ($400 total budget). When the first $50 minimum debt is gone, the next debt receives its $50 minimum + the original $100 extra = $150 extra. When that one’s gone, the third debt receives $300/month against its original minimum. This compounding effect is why both methods clear debt 30–60% faster than minimums alone, and why even small extra payments matter early in the plan.

Frequently Asked Questions

Avalanche vs Snowball — which is actually better?▾

Avalanche always wins on math: it pays off your highest-rate debt first and saves the most interest. Snowball wins on behavior: paying off small debts first creates fast wins and builds momentum. A 2012 study from Northwestern showed people on Snowball were more likely to actually finish their plan. Translation: Avalanche if you’re analytically motivated, Snowball if you need the dopamine. The best plan is the one you’ll stick with.

What is the Debt Avalanche method?▾

Pay the minimum on every debt every month, then put every extra dollar toward the debt with the highest interest rate. When that debt is gone, roll its full payment (minimum + extra) into the next-highest-rate debt. Continue until all debts are gone. This minimizes total interest paid — typically saves $100–$2,000+ over Snowball depending on your debt mix.

What is the Debt Snowball method?▾

Pay the minimum on every debt every month, then put every extra dollar toward the debt with the smallest balance — regardless of interest rate. When the smallest is gone, roll its payment to the next smallest. The early wins create motivation, which is why Dave Ramsey popularized this approach. You may pay slightly more in interest but you’re more likely to finish.

Should I consolidate my debts instead?▾

Maybe. A consolidation loan or 0% balance-transfer card can drop your effective rate and simplify payments. Run the numbers: if the consolidation rate × loan term beats your current weighted-average rate × payoff time, consolidate. Watch for transfer fees (typically 3–5%), promo-rate expiry (usually 12–21 months), and discipline risk — many people consolidate, then run cards back up.

Can extra debt payments hurt my credit score?▾

Generally no — paying down balances is one of the fastest ways to raise your score because it reduces credit utilization. The only cases where it can dip your score: closing a paid-off card removes its credit limit (raising your utilization on remaining cards), and paying off the only installment loan reduces credit-mix diversity. Both effects are minor and temporary.

Should I pay off debt or build savings first?▾

Build a starter emergency fund of $1,000–$2,000 first — without it, the next car repair becomes new debt. Then attack high-interest debt (anything above ~7%) aggressively. Lower-rate debt (mortgage, federal student loans) can run alongside saving for retirement, especially if you’re missing employer 401(k) match — that match is an instant 100% return that no debt rate beats.

How does paying off debt affect my debt-to-income ratio?▾

Each debt you eliminate raises your borrowing capacity. As a rough rule, every $100/mo of recurring debt eliminated boosts the home price you qualify for by ~$20,000 at current mortgage rates. If you’re house-shopping, prioritize paying off debts that have ≥ 10 monthly payments remaining (lenders ignore debts with < 10 payments left when calculating DTI).

Where can I find extra money to pay debt?▾

The four reliable sources, ranked by impact: (1) Cancel subscriptions and lifestyle creep — review last 90 days of statements; (2) Income side: ask for a raise or take on freelance work — even $200/mo extra cuts a 10-year payoff by 2+ years; (3) Tax refund or bonus: redirect 100% to debt rather than absorbing into spending; (4) Sell what you don’t use — that exercise bike, the second car, the camera you haven’t touched in two years.
