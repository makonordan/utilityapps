How Loan Amortization Works

Every monthly payment is split between interest (the lender’s cost) and principal (the part that actually pays down your loan). The total is fixed; the split is not.

  • Early in the loan: most of each payment goes to interest because the balance is large.
  • Mid-loan: the split equalizes — roughly half principal, half interest.
  • Late in the loan: most of each payment goes to principal because the balance is small.

Loan Type Cheat Sheet

  • Personal loans — unsecured, fixed-rate, 2–7 year terms. Used for debt consolidation, medical bills, and large one-off purchases. Rates tied closely to credit score.
  • Auto loans — secured by the vehicle. New-car rates run lower than used-car rates because of resale value certainty. Terms now stretch to 84+ months — be cautious, that\'s a lot of interest.
  • Student loans — federal loans have fixed rates, flexible repayment, and forbearance options. Private loans price like personal loans: better with a cosigner, refinanceable once you\'re working.
  • Business loans — wide rate spread (6–30%) depending on revenue, time in business, and collateral. SBA-guaranteed loans cap at 7-year terms with rates currently ~10–13%.

The Power of an Extra $50/Month

Even small extra payments create outsized savings because they shorten the time interest has to compound on the remaining balance.

Example: $25,000 personal loan at 9% APR for 5 years.

  • Standard payment: $519/mo, $6,135 total interest, paid off month 60.
  • + $50/month extra: paid off month 53 — 7 months earlier — and saves about $720 in interest. That extra $50 × 53 months ($2,650 of out-of-pocket principal) bought you $720 of interest savings — a 27% return on the prepaid dollars.
  • + $200/month extra: paid off month 41 — 19 months earlier — and saves about $2,000 in interest.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is my monthly loan payment calculated?▾

Standard amortization formula: M = P × [r(1+r)^n] ÷ [(1+r)^n − 1], where P is loan amount, r is the monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12), and n is the number of monthly payments. The result is fixed every month — what changes is the principal/interest split. Early payments are mostly interest; late payments are mostly principal.

What is APR and how is it different from the interest rate?▾

The interest rate is the cost of borrowing the principal. APR (Annual Percentage Rate) wraps in upfront fees — origination, discount points, certain closing costs — and re-expresses everything as a single annualized rate. APR is the better number for comparing offers from different lenders. The TILA disclosure your lender provides shows both.

What loan amount can I qualify for?▾

Lenders look at three things: credit score (typically 660+ for prime rates, 720+ for the best rates), debt-to-income ratio (your total monthly debts ÷ gross monthly income, ideally under 36%), and stable income/employment history. Personal loan amounts typically max at $50–100k for prime borrowers; auto loans can go higher when secured by the vehicle.

Should I take a longer term to lower my monthly payment?▾

Be careful — a longer term lowers the monthly payment but raises total interest substantially. A $25,000 loan at 8% for 3 years costs $3,194 in interest; the same loan stretched to 7 years costs $7,790. The "lower payment" looks like a win until you realize you handed the lender an extra $4,600. Pick the shortest term you can comfortably afford.

How much does my credit score affect the rate?▾

Significantly. On a personal loan, a 740 FICO might land you 7% APR while a 640 FICO sees 18%. On a $20,000 5-year loan, that gap is $9,500 in extra interest. Auto loans show similar tiers. If your score is below 680, often the highest-impact financial move is delaying the loan 6–12 months while you improve credit.

What's the difference between secured and unsecured loans?▾

Secured loans (auto loans, mortgages, home equity) are backed by collateral — fail to pay and the lender takes the asset. Lower rates because the lender's risk is lower. Unsecured loans (personal loans, credit cards, most student loans) have no collateral; the lender can sue you but can't seize a specific asset. Higher rates to compensate. Cosigners can effectively turn an unsecured loan secured-by-someone-else.

Can I pay off a loan early without penalty?▾

Most modern personal, auto, and student loans in the U.S. have no prepayment penalty — you can pay extra principal or pay off the entire balance early and save interest. A few subprime auto loans and older mortgages had "precomputed interest" or prepayment penalties; check your loan disclosure. The CFPB requires lenders to disclose prepayment penalties prominently.

What's a healthy debt-to-income ratio for a loan?▾

Lenders prefer total back-end DTI under 36–43% (all monthly debts ÷ gross monthly income). For a single new loan, a payment under 20% of monthly income is comfortable; 20–35% is workable but tight; over 35% is high-risk and usually means the loan is too large or the term too short. The DTI panel in this calculator gives a quick check on the loan you're modeling.

Should I consolidate multiple loans into one?▾

Consolidation makes sense when (1) the new rate is meaningfully lower than your weighted-average current rate, (2) the term doesn't balloon (a longer term can erase the rate savings), and (3) any consolidation fees are recouped well within the new term. Common scenarios: a personal loan paying off high-rate credit cards, or refinancing private student loans when rates drop.

Are loan calculations the same in every country?▾

The amortization math is universal. What varies: APR disclosure rules (the U.S. TILA, EU Consumer Credit Directive, UK FCA rules each define APR slightly differently), prepayment penalty regulations, default interest rules, and which fees count toward APR. This calculator computes the loan-payment math itself, which is identical worldwide; verify local fee/regulation specifics with your lender.
