How to Read a Loan Comparison

The three numbers worth obsessing over when comparing loans:

  • Monthly payment — what you owe every month. This determines whether you can comfortably afford the loan.
  • Total interest paid — the lifetime cost premium for borrowing. The most underweighted number in mortgage shopping.
  • Total cost (principal + interest) — the absolute amount of money you hand to the lender over the life of the loan. The single best apples-to-apples number.

Don’t forget the break-even point: if Loan A has a lower rate but higher upfront fees, you only come out ahead if you keep the loan past the break-even date.

Common Comparison Scenarios

  • Two lenders, same product: same term and product type (e.g. both 30-year fixed). Lower rate almost always wins — but check origination fees.
  • 15-year vs 30-year: 15-year wins on total cost; 30-year wins on cash-flow flexibility. Use the Break-Even Insight in this calculator to quantify the tradeoff.
  • Buy-down vs no points: a rate buy-down (paying points) lowers the rate but costs cash today. Compare the lifetime savings to the upfront cost.
  • Conventional vs FHA: FHA may have a lower rate but tacks on lifetime mortgage insurance. The all-in monthly cost (PI + MI) is the right comparison number, not just the headline rate.
  • Refinance offers: closing costs typically run $3–7k. The break-even in months is closing costs ÷ monthly savings. Use the dedicated refinance calculator for full break-even analysis.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know which loan is the better deal?▾

Look beyond the monthly payment. The lowest monthly payment usually means a longer term, which often costs more in total interest. The most reliable single number is total cost over the life of the loan (principal + interest + any required fees). When two loans have different terms, also weigh how long you’ll actually keep the loan — refinancing or selling early changes which loan wins.

Does the lowest monthly payment always win?▾

No. A 30-year loan at 7% has a lower monthly payment than a 15-year loan at 6% on the same balance, but the 30-year option can cost ~2× more in interest over its life. Lower monthly = more flexibility; lower total cost = more wealth long-term. Pick based on your cash-flow situation and how long you plan to hold the loan.

What’s the difference between APR and interest rate?▾

The interest rate is the cost of borrowing the principal. APR (Annual Percentage Rate) wraps in upfront costs like origination fees, discount points, and certain closing costs, then re-expresses everything as a single annualized rate. APR is closer to the “true cost” of the loan and is the better number for comparing offers from different lenders.

How do origination fees and closing costs affect the comparison?▾

They can flip the winner. A loan with a 0.25% lower rate but $4,000 in extra closing costs may not break even for 5+ years. Always compare the Loan Estimate (Page 3, Comparisons section) which shows total cost in 5 years and total interest paid as a percentage. This calculator focuses on the rate-and-term comparison; layer fees on top to get the full picture.

Should I choose a 15-year or 30-year mortgage?▾

A 15-year term has a 0.5–0.75% lower rate on average, builds equity ~3× faster, and saves enormous amounts in interest — but the monthly payment is roughly 50% higher. Choose 30 years if you need cash flow flexibility (and consider making extra principal payments). Choose 15 years if you can comfortably afford the higher payment and want guaranteed forced savings.

What is a discount point and is it worth buying?▾

One discount point costs 1% of the loan amount upfront and typically lowers your rate by ~0.25%. Break-even is usually 4–7 years. Worth it if (1) you’ll keep the loan past break-even, (2) you can pay points without reducing your reserves below 6 months, and (3) you’ve already maxed out negotiating room on the rate itself. If you might refinance or move within 5 years, points usually don’t pay off.

Does my credit score affect both loan offers equally?▾

Different lenders weight credit tiers differently — a 720 vs. 760 score can change one lender’s rate by 0.125% and another’s by 0.5%. Loan programs also matter: FHA is more credit-tolerant; jumbo and portfolio loans much less so. Always shop the same scenario (same credit pull, same down payment, same property) across 3+ lenders within a 14-day window so the inquiries count as one under FICO scoring.

Should I lock in my rate?▾

Lock once you’ve chosen the lender and have an executed purchase contract (or for a refinance, once you’re ready to commit). Standard locks are 30–60 days; longer locks cost more. If rates are trending up, lock immediately. If trending down, ask about a one-time float-down option — many lenders allow a single rate re-shop before closing.
