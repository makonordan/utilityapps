When Refinancing Makes Sense

  • Break-even under 24 months — almost always a clear win, especially if rates may rise further. You recoup costs quickly and every month after is pure savings.
  • Break-even 24–60 months — worthwhile if you’re confident you’ll stay in the home and keep the loan for at least that long.
  • Break-even over 60 months — only refinance if you plan to stay long-term. Selling or refinancing again before break-even is a loss.

Other valid reasons unrelated to rate: switching from ARM to fixed, removing PMI, shortening the term, or accessing equity (cash-out).

When Refinancing Doesn’t Make Sense

  • You’re moving in 2–3 years. Even a great rate won’t recoup closing costs in time.
  • You restart a 30-year clock late in your existing loan. Refinancing year 22 of a 30-year into a fresh 30-year means more total interest unless the rate cut is very large.
  • The new payment stretches your budget. Cash-out and longer terms feel good monthly but increase risk and total cost.
  • Your credit score has dropped. You may qualify but at a worse rate than you have now. Build the score back up before refinancing.

How to Get the Best Refinance Rate

  1. Shop at least 3 lenders. Rates can vary 0.25–0.5% on the same application. The CFPB found borrowers who shop save an average of $1,500 over the life of the loan.
  2. Apply within 14 days. Multiple mortgage inquiries in a 14–45 day window count as one inquiry under FICO scoring. Don’t spread applications out.
  3. Get Loan Estimates, not quotes. The standardized 3-page Loan Estimate makes apples-to-apples comparison easy. Page 3 shows total cost in 5 years and APR — both better numbers than headline rate.
  4. Lock when you’re ready. Rates change daily. Standard locks are 30–60 days; negotiate a float-down option if rates may drop further.
  5. Negotiate fees, not just rate. Origination, processing, and underwriting fees are often padded. Ask for a fee waiver — many lenders will give you $500–$1,000 off to win the deal.

Frequently Asked Questions

When does refinancing make sense?▾

The classic rule of thumb is a 1% rate reduction, but the only number that actually matters is your break-even month — closing costs ÷ monthly savings. If you’ll keep the loan past that month, refinancing makes sense. If you’re selling or refinancing again before then, you’re losing money. Most "should I refi" advice ignores this and just compares rates.

How is the break-even point calculated?▾

Break-even month = total closing costs ÷ monthly payment savings. If your refi saves $200/mo and costs $4,000 in closing, you break even at month 20. After that, every month is pure savings. This calculator computes it from the inputs you provide and visualizes it on the chart — the month where the green cumulative-savings line crosses the red closing-costs line.

What are typical refinance closing costs?▾

Closing costs typically run 2–5% of the loan amount in the U.S., or about $3,000–$7,000 on a $250,000 loan. Major components: origination fee (~0.5–1% of loan), appraisal ($400–$700), title insurance ($500–$2,000), credit report (~$50), recording fees ($50–$200), and any discount points you choose to buy. Some lenders offer "no-closing-cost" refinances — they bake the costs into a higher rate instead.

Should I "no-closing-cost" refinance?▾

It’s rarely free — the lender either rolls costs into the loan balance (you pay them with interest over 30 years) or charges a higher rate. Run both options through this calculator: the no-cost version usually has a worse break-even past 5–7 years. Use no-cost if you’re confident you’ll move or refi again within 3–4 years; otherwise paying upfront is cheaper.

Will refinancing hurt my credit score?▾

Slightly, briefly. The credit pull is a hard inquiry — typically a 5-point drop that recovers in 3–6 months. Mortgage shopping inquiries within a 14–45 day window count as a single inquiry under FICO, so apply with multiple lenders quickly. The much bigger long-term factor is keeping payments current on the new loan.

Should I refinance to a shorter term?▾

If you can afford the higher payment, often yes. A 30-year loan refinanced into a 15-year typically saves enormous total interest (often $80,000+ over the life of the loan on a $300k mortgage) and builds equity ~3× faster. Run both options in this calculator: the 15-year monthly payment is higher, but total cost is dramatically lower.

What is a cash-out refinance?▾

A cash-out refi takes a new loan larger than your current balance and pockets the difference. Useful for high-interest debt consolidation or large home improvements, but you’re trading unsecured debt for debt secured by your home. Rates on cash-out refis are typically 0.125–0.5% higher than rate-and-term refis. Lenders cap loan-to-value at 80% for most cash-out refis (95% for VA).

How long does refinancing take?▾

Typically 30–45 days from application to closing in the U.S. Steps: apply (1 day), document gathering (1–2 weeks), appraisal (1 week), underwriting (1–2 weeks), closing prep (1 week). To speed it up: have 2 years of W-2s, 2 months of pay stubs, 2 months of bank statements, and your insurance declarations page ready before applying.
