How to Calculate Percentages — Complete Guide

What Is a Percentage?

A percentage is a number expressed as a fraction of 100. The word comes from the Latin per centum, meaning “out of a hundred.” Writing 45% is exactly the same as writing 45/100 or 0.45 — three equivalent ways to say the same thing. Percentages are used universally because they provide a common scale for comparison: whether you’re looking at exam scores, price discounts, tax rates, or profit margins, everything lands on the same 0–100 (or beyond) scale.

1. Finding X% of YUse calculator →

This is the most common percentage calculation — used for tips, tax, discounts, commissions, and more.

Result = (Percentage ÷ 100) × Number

Example: 15% of 200. Divide 15 by 100 to get 0.15, then multiply by 200: 0.15 × 200 = 30. A quick mental shortcut: 10% is always the number with one decimal place removed (10% of 200 = 20), then adjust from there.

2. X is What Percent of Y?Use calculator →

Used for test scores, budget tracking, and sales attainment — any situation where you know the part and the whole and need the percentage.

Percentage = (Part ÷ Whole) × 100

Example: you scored 78 out of 90. 78 ÷ 90 = 0.8667, then × 100 = 86.67%. The result can exceed 100% — scoring 95 out of 90 on extra credit gives 105.6%, which is valid and simply means you exceeded the target.

3. Percentage ChangeUse calculator →

Measures how much a value changed relative to its starting point. The sign tells you the direction: positive = increase, negative = decrease.

% Change = ((New − Old) ÷ |Old|) × 100

Example: a stock price went from $50 to $75. Change = (75 − 50) ÷ 50 × 100 = +50%. Note the denominator is the absolute original value — this handles cases where the original is negative (like a loss turning into a gain).

4. Percentage Increase and DecreaseUse calculator →

When you know the original value and want to apply a percentage to find the new value, the multiplier method is the cleanest approach.

Increase

New = Original × (1 + %÷100)

Decrease

New = Original × (1 − %÷100)

A 25% increase on 200: 200 × 1.25 = 250. A 25% decrease on 200: 200 × 0.75 = 150. Important: a 25% increase followed by a 25% decrease does not return to the original (200 → 250 → 187.5), because the decrease applies to the larger base.

5. Discounts and Sale PricesUse calculator →

A discount is just a percentage decrease applied to a price.

Sale Price = Original × (1 − Discount% ÷ 100)

A £120 item at 25% off: £120 × 0.75 = £90. You save £30. Be careful with “stacked” discounts — two 20% discounts do not equal 40% off. They equal 1 − (0.80 × 0.80) = 1 − 0.64 = 36% off.

6. Adding and Removing TaxUse calculator →

Adding tax (VAT/GST) and removing it from a tax-inclusive price use different formulas — a common source of error.

Add Tax

Total = Price × (1 + Rate÷100)

Remove Tax

Price = Total ÷ (1 + Rate÷100)

$100 + 20% VAT = $120. Removing 20% VAT from $120: $120 ÷ 1.20 = $100. Never subtract 20% directly from $120 — that would give $96, which is wrong.

7. Profit Margin and MarkupUse calculator →

Margin and markup measure the same profit in dollars but express it relative to different bases — a critical distinction for pricing decisions.

Margin (÷ Revenue)

((Rev − Cost) ÷ Rev) × 100

Markup (÷ Cost)

((Rev − Cost) ÷ Cost) × 100

Cost $80, sell $120: profit = $40. Margin = 40÷120 × 100 = 33.3%. Markup = 40÷80 × 100 = 50%. Margin is always lower than markup for any positive profit because revenue (the larger number) is the denominator.

8. Tips and Bill SplittingUse calculator →

Tip = Bill × (Tip% ÷ 100)  |  Per person = (Bill + Tip) ÷ People

A $85 bill with a 20% tip: tip = $85 × 0.20 = $17. Total = $102. Split 4 ways: $102 ÷ 4 = $25.50 per person. Standard tipping in the US is 15–20% for sit-down restaurants; 10% is considered minimal for good service.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I calculate the percentage of a number?▶
Multiply the number by the percentage and divide by 100. Formula: (Percentage ÷ 100) × Number. For example, 15% of 200 = (15 ÷ 100) × 200 = 0.15 × 200 = 30.
What is percentage change?▶
Percentage change measures how much a value increased or decreased relative to its original value. Formula: ((New − Old) ÷ |Old|) × 100. A positive result means an increase; negative means a decrease. Example: going from 80 to 100 is a +25% change.
How do I calculate a discount?▶
Sale Price = Original Price × (1 − Discount% ÷ 100). For a 30% discount on £150: £150 × (1 − 0.30) = £150 × 0.70 = £105. You save £45.
How do I calculate tip at a restaurant?▶
Tip Amount = Bill × (Tip% ÷ 100). For a 15% tip on a $85 bill: $85 × 0.15 = $12.75. To split equally: divide the total (bill + tip) by the number of people.
What is profit margin vs markup?▶
Margin expresses profit as a percentage of the selling price (revenue). Markup expresses profit as a percentage of the cost. Same profit in dollars — different base. If cost is $80 and price is $120, margin = ($40÷$120)×100 = 33.3%, markup = ($40÷$80)×100 = 50%.
How do I convert a grade to a percentage?▶
Divide your score by the total possible marks and multiply by 100. Score ÷ Total × 100. Example: 45 out of 60 = (45÷60)×100 = 75%. A 75% typically corresponds to a C or C+ in the US grading system.
What is percentage difference?▶
Percentage difference is a symmetric measure of the gap between two values, using their average as the reference. Formula: |V1−V2| ÷ ((V1+V2)÷2) × 100. Unlike percentage change, order does not matter — swapping the values gives the same result.
How do I add tax to a price?▶
Multiply the pre-tax price by (1 + Tax Rate ÷ 100). For a $100 item at 20% VAT: $100 × 1.20 = $120. The tax amount alone is $100 × 0.20 = $20.
How do I remove tax from a price?▶
Divide the tax-inclusive price by (1 + Tax Rate ÷ 100). For a $120 total at 20% VAT: $120 ÷ 1.20 = $100 pre-tax price. Tax amount = $120 − $100 = $20. Never multiply by (1 − rate) — that gives the wrong answer.
How do I calculate percentage increase?▶
New Value = Original × (1 + Percentage ÷ 100). For a 25% increase on 80: 80 × 1.25 = 100. The multiplier method makes it a single step — no need to calculate the increase separately then add it.
How do I calculate percentage decrease?▶
New Value = Original × (1 − Percentage ÷ 100). For a 20% decrease on 150: 150 × 0.80 = 120. Note: a 20% increase followed by a 20% decrease does not return to the original — decreasing by 20% on a larger base removes more than the original increase added.
What is a good profit margin?▶
It depends entirely on the industry. Software and SaaS businesses commonly achieve 70–85% gross margins. Retail is typically 25–50%. Restaurants have 60–70% gross margins but only 3–9% net margins after labor and overheads. A "good" margin beats your industry average and covers all fixed costs comfortably.
How do I calculate GPA?▶
Convert each letter grade to grade points (A=4.0, B=3.0, etc.), multiply each by the course credit hours, sum all the results, then divide by total credit hours. Example: a 3-credit A (4.0×3=12) and a 3-credit B (3.0×3=9) gives GPA = (12+9)÷6 = 3.5.
What grade do I need on my final exam?▶
Use the formula: Needed Score = (Target Grade − Current Grade × (1 − Exam Weight÷100)) ÷ (Exam Weight÷100). Example: current grade 70%, target 75%, exam is 40% of final grade: (75 − 70×0.60) ÷ 0.40 = (75 − 42) ÷ 0.40 = 82.5%. You need at least 82.5% on the exam.
How do I convert a ratio to a percentage?▶
Divide the part by the total and multiply by 100. For the ratio 3:4, the calculation is (3÷4)×100 = 75%. If you have a part-to-part ratio like 3:1, first find the total (3+1=4), then 3÷4×100 = 75% for the first part.
What is the difference between percentage and percentile?▶
A percentage is a proportion expressed out of 100 — your test score is 85%. A percentile is a rank within a distribution — scoring in the 85th percentile means you scored higher than 85% of other test-takers. You could score 60% on a test but be in the 90th percentile if the test was very hard and most others scored lower.
