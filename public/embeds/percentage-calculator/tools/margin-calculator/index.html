Margin vs Markup — What's the Difference?

Margin and markup are two of the most commonly confused terms in business pricing — and the confusion is expensive. Using markup when you mean margin (or vice versa) can result in pricing that consistently undercuts your profitability targets, sometimes by a significant amount.

Profit Margin

Profit margin measures profit as a percentage of revenue (the selling price). It answers the question: “Of every dollar I receive from a customer, how many cents are profit?”

Margin = ((Revenue − Cost) ÷ Revenue) × 100

Example: you sell a product for $200 that cost you $120. Gross profit is $80. Margin = ($80 ÷ $200) × 100 = 40%. This means 40 cents of every dollar received is profit.

Markup

Markup measures profit as a percentage of cost. It answers: “How much have I added on top of what I paid?”

Markup = ((Revenue − Cost) ÷ Cost) × 100

Using the same example: $80 profit on a $120 cost. Markup = ($80 ÷ $120) × 100 = 66.7%. This means you charged 66.7% more than the cost.

Why They're Different — and Why It Matters

Both numbers describe the same $80 profit on the same transaction, but they express it relative to different bases. Margin will always be lower than markup for any positive profit, because revenue (the larger number) is in the denominator for margin, while cost (the smaller number) is in the denominator for markup.

The dangerous mistake:setting a target of “30% profit” and accidentally applying it as a 30% markup. If your cost is $100 and you add a 30% markup, you sell at $130 — but your margin is only $30 ÷ $130 = 23.1%, not 30%. Over thousands of transactions, this gap between the target and the actual margin compounds into significant revenue loss.

Converting Between the Two

Markup → Margin

Margin = Markup ÷ (100 + Markup) × 100

50% markup → 33.3% margin

Margin → Markup

Markup = Margin ÷ (100 − Margin) × 100

40% margin → 66.7% markup

Which Should You Use?

  • Use marginwhen talking to investors, accountants, and in financial reporting. Gross margin and net margin are the standard metrics in P&L statements.
  • Use markupwhen pricing products in retail or wholesale where you know the cost and need to set a price. A “keystone” markup (100%) simply doubles the cost.
  • Always clarifywhich measure you mean in internal discussions — “we need 40% on this product” is ambiguous without specifying margin or markup.

Gross Margin vs Net Margin

Gross margin only deducts the direct cost of goods sold (COGS) from revenue. Net margin deducts everything — COGS, operating expenses, interest, and taxes. A software company might show an 80% gross margin but a 15% net margin after salaries, servers, and marketing spend. For pricing decisions, gross margin is most relevant. For overall business health, net margin is the truer measure.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between margin and markup?▶
Margin (profit margin) expresses profit as a percentage of the selling price (revenue). Markup expresses profit as a percentage of the cost. Both measure the same profit in dollars — they just use a different base. A 50% markup is always a 33.3% margin. Margin is typically used in finance and pricing; markup is common in retail and wholesale.
How do I calculate profit margin?▶
Profit Margin = ((Revenue − Cost) ÷ Revenue) × 100. For example, selling a product for $250 that cost $150: Gross Profit = $100, Margin = ($100 ÷ $250) × 100 = 40%.
What is a good profit margin?▶
It depends on the industry. Software and SaaS businesses often achieve 70–85% gross margins. Retail typically sees 25–50%. Restaurants have 60–70% gross margins but only 3–9% net after labor and overheads. A "good" margin is one that exceeds your industry average and covers all fixed costs comfortably.
How do I convert markup to margin?▶
Margin = Markup ÷ (100 + Markup) × 100. For example, a 50% markup: 50 ÷ (100 + 50) × 100 = 33.3% margin. To go the other way: Markup = Margin ÷ (100 − Margin) × 100. A 40% margin: 40 ÷ (100 − 40) × 100 = 66.7% markup.
What is the formula to find selling price from cost and margin?▶
Selling Price = Cost ÷ (1 − Margin ÷ 100). For example, your cost is $120 and you want a 40% margin: $120 ÷ (1 − 0.40) = $120 ÷ 0.60 = $200. You would sell for $200, earning $80 profit — which is 40% of $200.
What is gross margin vs net margin?▶
Gross margin is calculated before operating expenses: it only subtracts the direct cost of goods sold (COGS) from revenue. Net margin subtracts all expenses — COGS, operating costs, interest, and taxes. A business can have a 60% gross margin and a 5% net margin if it has high rent, salaries, or advertising costs.
What is a 100% markup?▶
A 100% markup means you sell the item for double what you paid. If your cost is $50, a 100% markup gives a selling price of $100. The resulting profit margin is 50% — you keep half the revenue as profit.
How do I find the maximum cost to hit a target margin?▶
Max Cost = Selling Price × (1 − Target Margin ÷ 100). If you want a 30% margin selling at $200: Max Cost = $200 × (1 − 0.30) = $200 × 0.70 = $140. Your cost must stay at or below $140 to achieve that margin. Use the "Find Cost" tab in the calculator above.
