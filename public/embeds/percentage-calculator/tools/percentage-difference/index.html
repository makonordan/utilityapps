Percentage Difference vs Percentage Change

These two measures are frequently confused, but they serve very different purposes. Choosing the wrong one can produce misleading results.

When to Use Percentage Difference

Use percentage difference when you are comparing two independent values and neither is the natural reference point. Classic examples:

  • Comparing prices at two different stores
  • Measuring the spread between two survey results
  • Scientific experiments where two instruments give different readings
  • Comparing salaries at two companies without knowing which is “correct”

When to Use Percentage Change

Use percentage change when there is a clear “before” (old value) and “after” (new value). The old value is the reference point:

  • Stock price last year vs. this year
  • Your weight before and after a diet
  • Sales figures from Q1 to Q2

The Symmetry Property

|V1 − V2| ÷ ((V1 + V2) ÷ 2) × 100

Because the formula uses the average of the two values as its denominator, swapping V1 and V2 produces the same result. This is the defining property of percentage difference. Percentage change is not symmetric — going from 80 to 100 is a +25% change, but going from 100 to 80 is a −20% change.

Interpreting Large Values

When one value is close to zero, percentage difference can become very large or even undefined (if both values are zero, the denominator collapses). In practice, a result above 100% simply means the two values differ by more than their average — a meaningful signal that the gap is substantial relative to either value.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is percentage difference?▶
Percentage difference measures how far apart two values are, expressed as a percentage of their average. It is symmetric — unlike percentage change, neither value is treated as "old" or "new." Formula: |V1 − V2| ÷ ((V1 + V2) ÷ 2) × 100.
How is percentage difference different from percentage change?▶
Percentage change is directional: it measures how much a value changed from a specific starting point. Formula: (New − Old) ÷ |Old| × 100. Percentage difference is symmetric: it measures the gap between two values using their average as the reference. Use percentage change when one value is clearly "before" and the other is "after." Use percentage difference when the two values are independent measurements.
Can percentage difference be more than 100%?▶
Yes. If one value is close to zero and the other is large, the difference can exceed 100%. For example, comparing 1 and 100: |1 − 100| ÷ ((1 + 100) ÷ 2) × 100 = 99 ÷ 50.5 × 100 ≈ 196%. There is no upper theoretical limit.
Why does the formula divide by the average instead of one of the values?▶
Using the average (midpoint) ensures the result is the same regardless of which value you call V1 or V2. If you divided by V1, swapping V1 and V2 would give different answers — which is misleading when neither value is the natural reference point. The average treats both values equally.
How do I calculate the percentage difference between two prices?▶
Use the formula: |Price1 − Price2| ÷ ((Price1 + Price2) ÷ 2) × 100. For example, comparing a £35 and a £45 product: |35 − 45| ÷ ((35 + 45) ÷ 2) × 100 = 10 ÷ 40 × 100 = 25%. This tells you the prices differ by 25% relative to their average.
What does a 0% percentage difference mean?▶
A 0% percentage difference means the two values are identical. The absolute difference in the numerator is zero (|V1 − V2| = 0), so the result is 0 regardless of the denominator. This confirms the values are equal.
