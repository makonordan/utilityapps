Understanding Percentage Increase and Decrease

Percentage increases and decreases feel intuitive, but they hide a subtle asymmetry that catches many people out — in salary negotiations, pricing, and investment returns alike.

The Multiplier Method

The cleanest way to apply a percentage change is to convert it into a single multiplier and then multiply. An increase of X% uses the multiplier 1 + X/100; a decrease uses 1 − X/100.

Increase by 30%

Multiplier = 1 + 0.30 = 1.30

200 × 1.30 = 260

Decrease by 30%

Multiplier = 1 − 0.30 = 0.70

200 × 0.70 = 140

Why Increases and Decreases Are Not Symmetric

A 20% increase followed by a 20% decrease does not return to the starting value. After increasing 100 by 20% you have 120. A 20% decrease on 120 gives 96 — not 100. The decrease is applied to a larger base, so the absolute loss is greater. To reverse a percentage increase exactly, you need a smaller percentage decrease.

Reversal formula: Decrease needed = Increase ÷ (1 + Increase ÷ 100) × 100. For a 25% increase: 25 ÷ 1.25 × 100 = 20% decrease to return to the original.

Compounding Multiple Changes

When applying several percentage changes in sequence, multiply the multipliers together. A salary that increases 10% one year and 8% the next grows by a combined factor of 1.10 × 1.08 = 1.188 — an effective 18.8% total increase, not 18%. The order does not matter; multiplication is commutative.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the formula for percentage increase?▶
New Value = Original × (1 + Percentage ÷ 100). For example, a 25% increase on 80: New Value = 80 × (1 + 25 ÷ 100) = 80 × 1.25 = 100. The multiplier (1.25 for 25%) is the key — it combines the original and the increase into a single multiplication.
What is the formula for percentage decrease?▶
New Value = Original × (1 − Percentage ÷ 100). For example, a 20% decrease on 150: New Value = 150 × (1 − 0.20) = 150 × 0.80 = 120. For a 20% decrease the multiplier is 0.80 — you keep 80% of the original.
If I increase by 25% then decrease by 25%, do I get back to the original?▶
No. After a 25% increase on 100 you have 125. A 25% decrease on 125 gives 125 × 0.75 = 93.75 — not 100. To undo a 25% increase you need to decrease by 20% (not 25%). The exact reversal is: decrease % = increase ÷ (1 + increase ÷ 100) × 100.
How do I reverse a percentage increase?▶
Divide the new value by (1 + original increase ÷ 100). For example, a price is now £150 after a 25% increase: Original = 150 ÷ 1.25 = £120. This is the same formula used by the "Remove Tax" calculator for VAT-inclusive prices.
How do I calculate a salary increase?▶
New Salary = Current Salary × (1 + Increase% ÷ 100). Example: £35,000 with a 7% raise → £35,000 × 1.07 = £37,450. The increase amount alone is £35,000 × 0.07 = £2,450.
What is a 100% increase?▶
A 100% increase doubles the original value. The multiplier is 1 + 1.00 = 2. So a 100% increase on 50 gives 50 × 2 = 100. More generally, a 200% increase triples the value (multiplier = 3), and a 50% increase gives 1.5× the original.
